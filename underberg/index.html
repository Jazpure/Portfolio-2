Speculative Marketing Plan

Underberg

A strategy for introducing the world's great digestif to New York's restaurant and bar scene.

175 Years · 44 Botanicals · 20ml

Overview

Underberg is the platonic ideal of a digestif. Sold exclusively in small 20ml bottles, it is potent and unmistakable.

The ritual aspect of this product: twisting off the paper, taking the shot, keeping the cap, which you can redeem for rewards, combines with its genuine efficacy in terms of digestion. It's extremely popular in Europe, and this is why: it's fun, and it does its job.

In the US and New York specifically, Underberg hasn't caught on despite its potential. This plan outlines a strategy to change that.

The Opportunity

There is a class of product that feels like it has always existed, even to people encountering it for the first time. Underberg's aesthetic possesses this quality: you can read its heritage in a split second. It's familiar without being known.

Campari's surge in popularity offers a clear parallel. Before the cocktail revival of the early 2000s, Campari was a cult product. It had some fans, but it wasn't a household name. Aesthetically, it has similar advantages to Underberg: vintage Campari posters were sold in art shops to people who had never bought a bottle. The strong visual identity and legible heritage is one reason Campari exploded once there was access.

Other examples follow the same logic: Carhartt becoming streetwear. Champion becoming fashionable on the strength of its logo alone. Even PBR becoming more than just another cheap beer. The aesthetic did the introduction before the product did.

Underberg is similarly positioned. The gothic font, the brown paper, and the size give it an artifact quality. It is found, not bought, which is an enormous asset in New York, where authenticity and obscurity are valued.

Why New York
  • Because New York offers the highest ROI.
  • As a cultural export hub, adoption in NYC travels.
  • The city runs on an economy of cultural capital. People value and share discoveries.
  • The distribution infrastructure already exists. Underberg is present in NYC bars. The gap is in restaurants. As a digestif, ideally, it is consumed after a meal.

Strategy

The core strategic principle: position Underberg as a discovered object, not a marketed one. Since its niche hasn't been filled, it should grow organically.

Food & Drink
Restaurant Expansion
Target high end independent restaurants, particularly Italian and pizza focused concepts, where the digestif occasion is natural, and the post meal ritual already exists. Develop outreach to sommeliers and beverage directors. Position Underberg as the house digestif: low cost, memorable. Something a server can offer as a fun nightcap.
Food & Drink
Pizza!
New York is a pizza town, and Underberg is unexpectedly suited in terms of flavor and effect. People love pizza, but they don't love the carb crash. Partner with well-regarded NYC pizza spots for a limited co-promotion. Document the pairing through Instagram and small food and drink publications.
Visuals
Thrift Store T-Shirt Drop
Produce a small run of Underberg-branded T-shirts with the original logo and typography. Donate them anonymously to thrift stores across NYC: LES, Bushwick, Ridgewood, etc. Let them be found. Someone finds it, wears it, gets asked about it.
Visuals
Poster Campaign
Produce a series of posters in the visual language of vintage European product advertising. Wheat-paste or sticker placement in subway stations and on building walls. Posters should be desirable as objects. If they end up on apartment walls, that's success.
Word of Mouth
Targeted Promotion
Identify bartenders, chefs, food writers, and cultural tastemakers who already have credibility. Send product with no ask attached. A note explaining the history and ritual is sufficient. Avoid traditional influencer campaign mechanics. The product's appeal depends on not feeling like a campaign.
Word of Mouth
The Discovery Mechanism
The goal is for people to feel they found Underberg, not that they were sold it.

Poster Series

I'd like to work with an art director to fully realize the underlying concept behind these posters. They contrast with the brand's current aesthetic: less polished and more optimized for wheat-pasting, I think it's better if they have a guerrilla feel. Conversely, some of Underberg's advertisements from the 60s, 70s, and 80s are beautiful, refined, and would undoubtedly be effective.
